Three steps, zero flashcard grind

  1. 01

    Press play

    Watch anime like you always do — English subtitles on. AnimeVocab reads the hidden Japanese track on YouTube, or listens to the audio itself on Netflix and Crunchyroll.

  2. 02

    Judge one word

    When a common, level-appropriate word is spoken, the video pauses on a card: the word in romaji, its reading, meaning, and the exact line it came from. Know it, learn it, or ignore it — one keystroke.

  3. 03

    Reviews find you

    Words you're learning come back as quick memory checks in later episodes — spaced repetition timed to when you'd forget, without ever opening a flashcard app.

Built for people who'd rather watch than study

Romaji-first, beginner-proof

Every card leads with roman letters — taikutsu, not 退屈 — so you can learn from episode one, before you can read a single kana. Switch to kana-first or kanji-first whenever you're ready.

Listening Mode

Real-time transcription turns any video on any site into study material — cards from what's actually spoken, while your English subs stay on.

Knows what's worth pausing for

Frequency data and JLPT levels pick one genuinely useful word per line. No pausing on particles, names, or words you've already learned.

A dashboard you'll want to check

Streaks, hours watched, words by JLPT level, review pipeline — computed live from your own local data.

Private by architecture

No account, no analytics, no server watching you. Progress lives in your browser; the code is open source under AGPL. In Listening Mode, audio goes straight from your browser to the transcription API — nowhere else.

Free where it can be. Paid where it costs us.

Questions people actually ask

Can I really learn Japanese just by watching anime?

Not by watching alone — you need to notice words, understand them, and meet them again before you forget. That's the whole product: AnimeVocab makes you notice one useful word at a time, tells you what it means in the exact sentence you heard, and schedules it to reappear right before you'd forget it.

I can't read hiragana yet. Is that a problem?

No — that's the default audience. Cards lead with romaji (taikutsu) and show the kana and kanji alongside, so the writing systems soak in gradually while you learn by sound.

How does it work on Netflix, where there are no Japanese subs?

Listening Mode captures the tab's audio and transcribes it in real time. Cards come from the spoken Japanese within a second or two, while your English subtitles stay on screen for context. Included with Pro, or free with your own OpenAI API key.

Will it interrupt me constantly?

No. It shows at most one card per line, respects a cooldown between cards, caps cards per hour, and you can switch to a non-blocking notification mode — or turn it off per site.

What happens to my data?

It stays in your browser. No accounts, no analytics, no tracking. The extension is open source (AGPL), so this is verifiable, not a promise.
