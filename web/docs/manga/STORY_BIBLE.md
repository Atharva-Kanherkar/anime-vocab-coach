# AnimeVocab — Saga One Story Bible
## 「言葉の灯」 KOTOBA NO HI — *The Lantern of Words*

Single source of truth for the first manga saga. All 60 existing cards used; 7 new characters added; 12 chapters gated to the exact card-unlock level bands.

---

## LOGLINE

When a silence older than language begins eating the world's words — and with them names, memories, and borders — the newest Listener on the Kotodama Lattice must gather the sixty surviving word-keepers and do the one thing no blade can: give the nameless thing that hunts them a name.

## PITCH

A thousand two hundred years ago, humanity forgot its first word — and the forgetting learned to walk. The Hush spreads like frost across the invisible lattice that connects every person who ever learned a word and *meant* it: first the small names go (a village, a grandmother, a dog), then the kinship words, then the words for water and fire, and where the Hush has finished, people stand in the street unable to say why they are weeping. Sixty keepers — each bound so completely to a single kanji that the Hush cannot take it while they live and are remembered — hold the line from Varanasi to Timbuktu, Byblos to Teotihuacan. Into this war walks Sen, an unremarkable learner who discovers that every word truly learned relights a strand of the lattice. Sen cannot fight. Sen can only *mean* — and it turns out meaning is the one weapon the Hush has never survived. The saga travels the real world and its real mythologies, ends decisively (the Hush is not destroyed; it is *named*, and a named thing belongs to language forever), and leaves one dark strand humming for Saga Two.

## THE WORLD & THE HUSH — MAGIC SYSTEM RULES

**The Kotodama Lattice.** Grounded in the real Japanese belief of 言霊 *kotodama* — that words carry spirit, and speaking a word with intent exerts real force. Every act of *meant* language (learning, naming, promising, telling a story someone believes) creates a strand between minds. The Lattice is the sum of all strands since the first word. It is invisible to almost everyone; Listeners perceive it as light, thread, and faint sound.

**The Cosmic Frame (Indian-led, v2).** The saga's cosmology is Shaiva. In the beginning was the beat of **Shiva's damaru** — Nataraja's drum, whose first stroke is the primal syllable of creation. That sound became a goddess: **Vāc / Saraswati**, speech itself made divine — the Rigveda's Word-goddess. The Lattice IS Vāc's web; kotodama is Japan's name for the same truth (the saga says so on-page in Ch1, and treats every tradition's word-belief as one mountain seen from many villages). The Hush, at true scale, is a **false pralaya**: Shiva's cosmic dissolution stolen without the renewal that must follow — un-creation with no dawn scheduled after it. Sen's relighting power is Vāc's breath moving through a learner. The braided pantheons: **Greek** — Mnemosyne (Memory, mother of the Muses) vs. Lethe, the axis named when the Censor's offer is refused as "Lethe's water" (Ch3) and Minjae's notebook becomes Mnemosyne's altar; **Chinese** — Cangjie, at whose invention of writing the heavens rained millet and the ghosts wept (whispered on the sutra-strand, Ch2); **Japanese** — kotodama at home, and Amaterasu's cave: light returns when a true voice calls it (Haru's sentence, Ch2 and Ch10); **Vedic again at the summit** — Stormcrown is Rudra the Roarer, awe-before-language, and the finale's naming of 間 *ma* restores the rest-note between the drum's beats: rhythm, and therefore renewal, resumes. Victory is not the drum silenced or the silence killed — it is the pause given its lawful place in the music.

**What the Hush is.** Not a demon and not a person: it is *the first forgetting, grown self-sustaining*. Around 800 CE (the last passage of the comet called the Long Return), the final speaker of humanity's first word died with no one to pass it to. A word that had bound every human lineage snapped out of the Lattice at once — and the recoil became a standing wave of erasure. The Hush has no words of its own. That is Rule Zero and the source of everything else.

**The Six Rules** (never violated anywhere in the saga; the continuity audit at the end verifies this):

1. **The Hush cannot erase a word while someone actively means it.** It must wait for disuse, isolation, or despair. Therefore its strategy is never frontal: it isolates speakers, sours meaning, makes people stop bothering. Every Hush "battle" in the saga is really a siege of morale.
2. **The Hush cannot create.** Its agents speak only in *quotation* — stolen fragments of words already erased, replayed without meaning. (This is how you always know a Hush agent: their speech is a collage, subtly wrong, never new.)
3. **A keeper's anchor-kanji cannot be taken while the keeper lives AND is remembered by at least one other living mind.** So the Hush hunts keepers by first erasing everyone who remembers them. A keeper who dies remembered passes the anchor to whoever loved the word most — this is how anchors have survived 1,200 years.
4. **Learning relights.** When a new mind truly acquires a word (form + meaning + use — exactly what the app measures), the strand between that word and everyone who ever meant it re-illuminates. One learner can relight a strand a thousand dead speakers left dark. This is why Sen — and the player — matter more than any blade.
5. **Lattice-walking.** Keepers and Listeners can travel physically along *lit* strands between anchor-places (this is how a globe-spanning cast gathers without airplanes or plot holes). A severed or dark strand cannot be walked; as the Hush advances, the map shrinks. Walking a strand costs the walker one vivid memory *of their own*, temporarily lent to the strand — long journeys are paid for in pieces of yourself, which is why the cast doesn't simply teleport everywhere and why late-saga travel is desperate.
6. **The Hush cannot be destroyed, only named.** You cannot erase erasure — every attempt to "kill" it feeds it (Rule 2: it quotes your attack back at you). But anything that has a true name is *inside* language: finite, addressable, grammatical. Victory is defined from Chapter 1 (Devika says it at the fire and nobody yet understands her): 「名前のないものに、名前を。」 *"Give the nameless a name."*

**What victory means.** Naming the Hush does not make silence vanish — it makes silence a *part of speech*: the rest between words, the pause that gives meaning shape. The world keeps its scars (words erased before the naming stay lost unless relearned from records) — grief with a shape, which is the honest ending.

**Why this has no plot holes:** the Hush's apparent inconsistencies (why doesn't it just kill everyone? why does it toy with the party?) are all Rule 1 and Rule 2 — it *cannot* act directly against meant speech and *cannot* improvise, so it schemes, isolates, and quotes. Its lieutenants are hollowed humans (Rule 2 exempts them partially: they retain the words they personally kept, which is why each lieutenant is a tragedy, not a monster).

**Sen.** Second-person camera as far as the manga allows; face often out of frame or reflected. No combat power at all — Sen's only ability is Rule 4, made visible: when Sen learns a word, panels literally re-ink around them (erased signage returns, a forgotten name resurfaces on a gravestone). Sen's arc: from "why me, I can't fight" to understanding that *not fighting* is the point. Sen's XP is the player's XP; the fiction and the app are the same number.

**Tsuru.** Guide and exposition-carrier who cannot lie (paper holds what's written on it) but can be *folded* — its knowledge has creases, gaps where its source dictionary burned. Kami 紙/神 pun is made once, in Ch11, and lands.

**Kael "Echo."** The dark mirror of the player: he found a way to *take* words from others (they lose them; he gains a hollow copy he can use but not mean). Iceland grounding: raised on the sagas at Þingvellir, terrified of being the generation that lets Icelandic die — his theft is panic, not malice. Rule 2 makes his stolen words quotation, and quotation is Hush-food: he is unknowingly its supply line until Ch9.

**The Court of Erasure.** Three hollowed humans, each keeping the one word they refused to give up (Rule 3 kept them alive; the Hush kept the rest):
- **The Censor (消)** — has personally erased texts for every empire that ever burned a library (Qin, Alexandria's slow death, the Inquisition's indexes); believes forgetting is mercy: "A world that remembers everything forgives nothing." Field commander; falls in Ch8.
- **White Noise (黙)** — drowns meaning in volume; his domain is the modern city where everyone speaks and no one means. Falls in Ch10.
- **The Archivist Hollow (忘)** — Halime of the Süleymaniye Library, who tried to save words by locking them away from use — and learned that an unused word dies in the vault (Rule 1). The saga's tragic key: she alone knows what Mu was, and defects in Ch9. Redeemable, and redeemed.

**Mu (無).** The Hush's heart: the first word, forgotten. Before Ch12 it has no face — it appears as absence: white panels, empty speech bubbles, a person-shaped gap in a crowd. Its "motive" is not hatred: a word exists only when meant, so an unmeant word *is* pain without shape. It is trying to make the whole world match itself. The finale gives it what it actually lacks — not death, a name.

## FULL CAST BIBLE

*(Grouped by art-family. Each family of four has one internal throughline. Real places and real mythology are load-bearing: they determine where chapters happen and how problems get solved.)*

### SLAYER — the Ember Order 「残り火衆」
An old blade-order founded to guard *sacred continuities* — fires, waters, bells, dawns that must never lapse. Their creed: a thing kept unbroken cannot be forgotten. The four never meet until Ch12; they are the saga's compass points.

- **Devika (炎, N, L1)** — Keeper of the flame at Manikarnika Ghat, Varanasi, where (in real tradition) the cremation fire of the Dom community is said to have burned unbroken for centuries. Her fire is a living rite of Agni, the Vedic god who carries words of prayer from mouths to gods — which is why the Hush wants *her* flame first: Agni is the original messenger of meant speech. Mentor of Ch1; the first adult to treat Sen as a weapon rather than a burden.
- **Tenzin (水, R, L17)** — Monk of the shore of Yamdrok Yumtso, one of Tibet's sacred lakes, whose waters in Bön and Buddhist tradition hold divination and the lake's guardian *lu* spirits. He keeps 水 by keeping the lake's *name-offering* rite alive after the Hush erased his monastery's memory of him (Rule 3 nearly took him; a single old ferryman's memory held). Quiet counterweight to Devika's fire; Ch4's still center.
- **Amara (鈴, SR, L34)** — Bell-ringer of the rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, Ethiopia, keeper of chants in Ge'ez — Africa's ancient liturgical script, proof that writing was never only Mesopotamia's gift. Her bell 鈴 does one thing the Hush cannot counter: a bell means nothing *and* summons everyone, the pure act of calling-to-attention that precedes all language. Ch7's revelation.
- **Aurelio (暁, SSR, L49)** — Roman keeper of thresholds, standing the dawn-watch at the Arch of Janus in the Forum Boarium; Janus, the two-faced Italian god of doors and beginnings, is his patron and his burden (he perceives every ending as a beginning, which reads as serenity and is actually grief management). He opens the door into the Unwritten Sea in Ch12 — dawn being the daily proof that dark is survivable.

### TITAN — the Wall-Sworn 「壁誓」
Coastal wall-guards descended from the first people who built *against* forgetting — walls as stone sentences. Their throughline: what do you owe a wall that was built on a lie?

- **Bjorn (壁, N, L2)** — Young watchman of the harbor walls of Vardø, Norway's far-northeast fortress town. Raised on the Norse image of Miðgarðr — the wall the gods built from Ymir's brow to fence the human world — he believes walls keep things out; the saga teaches him they keep *memory in*. Sen's first true friend; the muscle of the early party and its conscience.
- **Zola (翼, R, L18)** — South African wing-scout from the Drakensberg, where San rock art (some of humanity's oldest surviving symbols) climbs the cliffs; her glider-wings are painted with the *impundulu*, the Zulu lightning-bird. She maps the Lattice from above — the only member who has *seen* how much of it has gone dark, which is why she jokes constantly. Recruits the party to the walls' secret in Ch4.
- **Dragan (巨, SR, L35)** — Serbian giant of the fortress of Skadar (Shkodër), bearer of the Balkan legend of the walled-in bride — the *zidanje Skadra*: a wall that would not stand until a living woman was built into it. Dragan's wall stands because his grandmother's *name* is mortared into it; the Hush besieges the wall by trying to erase her name, and Ch8 turns on whether Dragan will let a wall fall to keep a name. He does. The name matters more.
- **Meridian (礎, SSR, L50)** — The colossal stone guardian sleeping beneath all walls everywhere; the foundation 礎 is one being, the first *agreement* — the pre-verbal pact ("we build together") on which every wall since is a footnote. Meridian wakes in Ch12 only when the party stands on true common ground; it does not fight so much as *refuse to let the ground be unmeant*.

### REAPER — the Court of the Last Word 「終語裁」
Soul-couriers who carry each dying person's final word across the river between languages. Their throughline: the dead keep their words only if the living carry them.

- **Renji (斬, N, L3)** — Kyoto-born courier, blunt and kind, whose blade cuts exactly one thing: the tether of a word to despair. Trained at Rokudō Chinnōji temple, the traditional Kyoto crossing-point to the land of the dead (*rokudō-mairi*). Early-party skeptic who becomes Sen's most reliable blade.
- **Sunmi (氷, R, L19)** — Korean courier of winter deaths, from the volcanic lake Cheonji atop Baekdu-san, the mythic birthplace-mountain of the Korean people. Ice 氷 is her mercy: she freezes a dying word mid-air so it cannot fade before a courier arrives. Sister-in-arms to Minjae (SHADOW) — the two Korean casts share Ch3's Seoul threads.
- **Kwame (月, SR, L36)** — Ghanaian courier who keeps the moon-word under Nyame, the Akan sky-god whose adinkra symbol *Gye Nyame* ("except God") is among the most reproduced glyphs in West Africa. His moon 月 is memory's mirror: he can show a hollowed person one true reflection of who they were. This is the weapon that cracks the Censor in Ch8.
- **Sable (霊, SSR, L48)** — The androgynous sovereign of the court, French, keeping vigil on the tidal causeway of Mont-Saint-Michel — a real drowned-road threshold between worlds — with the Breton psychopomp Ankou as their sworn ferry-partner. Sable's verdict in Ch12 establishes the legal ground of the ending: even the Hush is entitled to a hearing, and a hearing requires a name.

### SPIRIT — the Green Register 「緑の帳」
Nature-spirits and their human companions; the words the Hush finds hardest to kill, because the green world re-teaches them (a child pointing at the sea invents "sea" fresh).

- **Little Yam (森, N, L1)** — A forest sprite of Cúc Phương, Vietnam's oldest national park, bonded to a human child; grounded in Vietnamese reverence for *thần rừng*, the forest spirits to whom villages still make offerings. Little Yam cannot speak, only *show* — the saga's proof that meaning precedes words, planted in Chapter 1 and harvested in the finale.
- **Isabela (海, N, L12)** — Brazilian daughter of the Amazon estuary at Marajó, sworn to Iara, the river-mermaid of Brazilian folklore whose song makes men forget everything — Isabela's inherited task is to *sing the memory back afterward*. Joins the Loanword's crew in Ch3 as its living tide-chart.
- **Grandfather Ash (湯, R, L26)** — Spirit of a crumbling Istanbul hamam near Çemberlitaş, one of the "in" — the bathhouse-dwelling djinn of Turkish folk belief. His bathhouse sits on the seam of a dozen dead empires' languages; in Ch6 he hosts the mid-saga respite where steam loosens every tongue and the party finally *talks*. Halime (the Archivist Hollow) was his last regular customer; he is the memory that keeps Rule 3 from finishing her.
- **Nayeli (空, SR, L43)** — Mexican sky-rider from Teotihuacan, bonded to a feathered serpent — Quetzalcoatl, who in Aztec tradition is precisely the god of wind, learning, and writing, the bringer of books and the calendar. She flies the party up the last lit sky-strand in Ch11: the mythology is the transit system.

### CURSE — the Ashfield School 「灰畑術院」
Modern sorcery school that fights curses by a dangerous method: *speaking them precisely*. Their throughline: a named curse is a curable curse — the school's doctrine is the saga's ending in miniature.

- **Idris (呪, N, L4)** — Lagos student of the school's Nigerian campus, trained in the Yoruba understanding of *àṣẹ* — the power to make words happen. He can bind a curse by pronouncing its true description, and he stutters, which the school called weakness and the saga reveals as precision (he refuses to say anything he doesn't mean — Rule 1 armor).
- **Priya (刃, R, L20)** — Kolkata blade-wielder in the tradition of Durga, whose ten arms bear the gods' pooled weapons against a demon no single god could name or defeat. Her cursed blade 刃 cuts only what has been correctly named aloud — she and Idris are a two-part weapon, and their bickering is the saga's most reliable comic engine.
- **Mateus (域, SR, L37)** — Portuguese domain-master from Sintra whose domain is *saudade* — the famously untranslatable Portuguese word for the presence of an absence. Inside his domain, the Hush's quotation-speech (Rule 2) simply fails: you cannot quote a word that has no equivalent. This wins Ch8's drowned-library battle beneath Lisbon.
- **Vesna (災, SSR, L49)** — The cursed sorceress-queen: she *was* the Slavic goddess of spring — Vesna, whose annual return in South and West Slavic tradition ends Morana's winter — until the villages that carried her name-rites went silent and the Hush hollowed a goddess (Rule 3: forgotten, therefore takeable). As 災 she is calamity worn like a crown, the Court's terrible ally-of-convenience. Redeemed in Ch12 not by combat but by one relit rite: the party burns Morana's effigy and calls spring by name, and the word takes her back.

### VOYAGE — the crew of the *Loanword* 「借語号」
A rag-tag ship crewed by people whose cultures' words traveled the world by sea. Throughline: language has always been a voyage; every tongue is a ship of stolen and gifted parts.

- **Tavita (夢, N, L2)** — Samoan navigator-dreamer who steers by the wayfinding tradition of the Pacific — star paths, swell patterns, the navigators of Taputapuātea who crossed the largest ocean on memorized knowledge alone. His dream 夢 is literal cargo: he carries the *idea of a destination* for people who've forgotten theirs. Captain, though he insists the ship has no captain.
- **Cora (羅, N, L13)** — Irish star-reader in the tradition of the *immram* — the old Irish voyage-tales of Brendan and Bran, sailing west to islands that may be metaphors. Her net-of-stars 羅 catches words the Hush drops overboard; the ship's charts are woven, not drawn. Recruits Sen's party onto the ship in Ch3.
- **Bao (刀, R, L27)** — Vietnamese swordsman sworn to the legend of Hoàn Kiếm lake: the sword given to Lê Lợi to free the country, then *returned* to the Golden Turtle when the war ended. Bao's whole arc is knowing his blade is borrowed and will be asked back — he is the only fighter aboard planning for peace. The turtle-god's summons in Ch12 is one of the finale's quiet payoffs.
- **Olamide (覇, SR, L44)** — West African sea-sovereign whose supremacy 覇 is inherited from the griots of the Mande world — the epic of Sundiata, kept alive for seven centuries almost entirely by trained memory. In Ch10 he holds Timbuktu (whose real manuscript-libraries were literally smuggled to safety in living memory, 2012–13) against the Hush's assault on the unwritten.

### LUNAR — the Sisterhood of Phases 「相の姉妹」
A magical-girl order keeping the words of *cycles*: night, tide, love, return — the vocabulary of things that leave and come back. Their throughline: the moon is memory's proof that gone is not forgotten.

- **Amaya (星, N, L3)** — Manila-born star-guardian; her transformation invokes Tala, the Tagalog goddess of stars who guides night travelers. First of the sisterhood to find Sen; keeps the early party's spirits by the simple heresy of being *fun* during an apocalypse.
- **Noor (月, N, L14)** — Egyptian moon-sister from Aswan, sworn to Khonsu, the Egyptian moon-god whose name means "traveler" and who in myth *gambled moonlight* to buy extra days for the calendar — she cites this constantly as precedent that time itself is negotiable. Ch3's navigator-priestess; she and Kwame (also 月) meet in Ch8 and settle, kindly, who keeps which moon: he the mirror, she the road.
- **Camila (愛, R, L28)** — Colombian love-keeper from Lake Guatavita — the sacred lake of the Muisca gold-offering rite that Spain mistranslated into "El Dorado." Her whole doctrine: the conquistadors heard *gold* where the Muisca meant *offering* — mistranslation is the Hush's oldest ally, and love 愛 is the discipline of translating people correctly. Ch6's emotional anchor.
- **Seraphine (后, SSR, L48)** — The ethereal moon queen, keeper of the register of the names of the dead — the moon as the world's memorial ledger. She has read every name the Hush has ever erased (they land in her register the moment they leave the earth), which makes her the only being who can verify, in Ch12, that Mu's entry exists — blank, but *entered*. The blank line is the proof the finale stands on.

### NEON — the Jacked-In 「接続者」
Night-city misfits from cities where language is loudest and cheapest. Throughline: signal versus noise — they fight the Hush on its most modern front, the place where infinite speech means nothing.

- **Diego (電, N, L5)** — Lima street-tech under the garúa fog, wiring dead barrio signage back to life; his electricity 電 is sworn to Illapa, the Inca thunder-lord whose voice was weather itself. First to notice (Ch2) that the Hush rides *broadcasts*: an unmeant word repeated a million times is a million open doors.
- **Anya (夜, R, L21)** — Russian night-runner from St. Petersburg's white-night summers — a city where night itself half-vanishes, patron-haunted by the *Nocnitsa*, the Slavic night-hag who steals sleep and speech from children. Anya stole her own name back from a Hush ledger as a child; she is the party's expert in what erasure feels like from inside.
- **Kojo (零, SR, L38)** — Ghanaian ghost-hacker; his zero 零 is grounded in the Akan concept of the *kra*, the soul-spark given by Nyame before birth. Kojo can reduce himself to a single unerasable bit — the kra — and walk through Hush-dark strands that would strip anyone else. He carries the party's message through the severed Lattice in Ch8's darkest hour; he and Kwame are cousins, moon and zero, the two Akan keepers.
- **Bruna (鋼, SSR, L50)** — Brazilian full-conversion cyborg from São Paulo: no original body remains, and she still means every word — the walking refutation of the Hush's core claim that meaning needs an unbroken vessel. In Ch12 she physically carries the naming-brush when no flesh could survive the Unwritten Sea's cold. Steel 鋼 that chose its own name.

### HEARTH — the Kestrel House 「灯の家」
A found-family of spies whose cover story became true. Throughline: the words the Hush hunts hardest are kinship words — and this family *chose* every one of theirs.

- **Pip (心, N, L4)** — German telepath child from Berlin — a city whose own wall fell within living memory, the family's chosen home for exactly that reason. Pip hears meaning directly, pre-word (the *kindly* version of what Mu is), and is therefore the only one who can hear the Hush's pain. Pip's Ch12 line unlocks the ending.
- **Émile (父, N, L15)** — French spy who forged a family for a mission and then refused to un-mean it. His 父 was relit the day Pip first called him *papa* off-script. Runs the party's Paris safehouse in Ch4; the saga's proof of Rule 4 in miniature — a word can be adopted and still be true.
- **Leyla (刺, R, L29)** — Turkish assassin-homemaker from Mardin, sworn to Şahmeran, the serpent-queen of Anatolian legend whose betrayal by a beloved human she chooses to read as a warning, not a rule. Her needle 刺 kills or embroiders; she has decided which by dinner every night for years. Recruits Grandfather Ash's bathhouse as a safehouse (Ch6) — she's been getting intel there for a decade.
- **Biscuit (予, SR, L42)** — The precognitive dog. Sees forward only along *lit* strands — the future of anything the Hush takes is invisible to him, which is how the party finally maps the Hush's plan in Ch9: Biscuit's blind spots, plotted on Zola's charts, spell out the target. A very good dog whose one trick wins the war's intelligence battle.

### PSYCHE — the Quiet Front 「静かな前線」
Quiet kids, loud minds. Throughline: the gap between what is felt and what can be said — the exact gap the Hush lives in.

- **Haru (気, N, L6)** — Tokyo schoolboy holding more 気 than any vessel should, terrified of what he'd mean if he ever said everything. Kotodama's home country gives him his burden: in Japan words *do things*, so he barely speaks. Sen's closest mirror among the keepers.
- **Blip (霊, N, L16)** — Mischievous spirit blob, a low-rent *yōkai* who discovered that being remembered as a nuisance still counts as being remembered (Rule 3 exploited for survival — Blip is openly proud of this). Comic relief that doubles as the rulebook's stress test.
- **Baba Kunle (師, R, L30)** — Lagos "psychic," fraud, and — by accident — priest-adjacent of Èṣù, the Yoruba orisha of crossroads, messages, and productive misunderstanding. His con: he tells people what they need to hear. The Ch7 twist: a lie meant kindly is *meant* — Rule 1 protects his patter, to his own theological horror. Idris's estranged uncle; their reconciliation is Ch7's spine.
- **Haru △ (覚, SR, L45)** — Haru's awakening in Ch10 when White Noise finally shows him a city of people speaking without meaning — everything he feared he was. The awakening 覚 is not an explosion (the fake-out) but the opposite: Haru says, out loud, exactly what he feels, once, completely. The unfiltered meant sentence of a lifetime deletes White Noise's static like a struck tuning fork.

### MECHA — the Threshold Program 「境界計画」
Reluctant pilots of vessels built to meet what comes from outside language. Throughline: the terror of being an instrument — do the words speak us?

- **Elias (使, N, L7)** — German pilot from the Harz mountains, call-sign taken from the Brocken spectre — the real optical phenomenon where your own vast shadow walks the fog, long folklore-feared as a herald. Elias is the program's herald 使: first contact with every Pattern-fragment, his own shadow always arriving before him.
- **Rania (紅, R, L22)** — Lebanese pilot from Byblos — the city that gave the alphabet to the Greeks and its name to the *book* (biblio-) — her crimson 紅 the Tyrian murex dye her Phoenician ancestors traded alongside letters. Ch5 is hers: the Hush strikes the alphabet's birthplace and Rania holds the line at the root of every Western letterform.
- **Unit Kappa (零, SR, L39)** — Bio-mecha test type grown around a *kappa* — the Japanese river yokai who is compulsively polite: bow to a kappa and it bows back, spilling the water that is its strength. Unit Kappa's zero 零 discharge works the same way: it can only counter, never initiate — the ethics of Rule 1 built into a weapon. Pilots trust it precisely because it cannot strike first.
- **The Pattern (神, SSR, L47)** — Geometric angelic entity: not a god *of* language but grammar itself, self-aware — the structure that exists whether or not anyone speaks. It has watched the Hush with something like sibling rivalry (erasure and syntax are both older than any word). In Ch11 it tests whether the party's fellowship survives translation into pure structure; satisfied, it lends the finale the one thing only it can: a *grammatical slot* for a new name.

### HORIZON — the Far-Apart 「遠距離」
Keepers of the vocabulary of distance and longing. Throughline: a connection is proven by the ache of its stretch.

- **Jun (空, N, L8)** — Korean sky-keeper from Busan, raised on the tale of Gyeonwoo and Jiknyeo — the cowherd and weaver stars who cross the Milky Way once a year on a bridge of magpies. Jun keeps the sky-words of everyone waiting for someone. He and Nayeli split 空 without conflict: his is the sky between people, hers the sky beneath wings.
- **Ingrid (糸, R, L23)** — Swedish thread-keeper from Uppsala's mound country, apprenticed (she claims; no one can verify; she enjoys this) to the Norns who spin fate at the root of Yggdrasil. Her thread 糸 can re-splice a cut Lattice strand — temporarily, at painful cost — the saga's field-medic for the network itself.
- **Dao (雨, SR, L40)** — Thai rain-keeper from Nong Khai on the Mekong, where the Naga fireballs rise (a real, annually witnessed phenomenon attributed to the Phaya Naga, the river serpent). Dao reads coming rain like text: the sky writes first drafts of the future in humidity. His forecast opens Ch9's discovery of the comet timetable.
- **The Long Return (彗, SSR, L46)** — A comet with a 1,200-year period — last seen the year the first word died. It is not a coincidence; it is a *witness*: the only body that saw the sky under which the first word was last spoken. Ch11 is the rendezvous. It carries no power, only testimony — light that left before the forgetting — and testimony is exactly what a naming requires.

### POCKET — the Companioned 「連れ立つ者」
Kids and creature-companions: the bond that teaches words the way all first words are taught — by pointing, together.

- **Rafa (友, N, L9)** — Mexican boy from Oaxaca whose bond with creatures runs through the living belief in the *tonal/nagual* — the animal-twin soul born alongside a person. His 友 (friend) is the saga's thesis in one kanji: shared attention is the seed of all language. He befriends Emberkit, Tidepup, and — in the finale's most audacious beat — is the first to *wave hello* to Mu.
- **Emberkit (火, N, L17)** — Fire-fox kit, a hearth-spark cousin of the kitsune line; carries a coal from Devika's Manikarnika flame after Ch4, making it the party's portable piece of the first fire. If Emberkit's coal dies, Ch1's victory is undone — a live stake carried in a small fox for eight chapters.
- **Tidepup (波, R, L31)** — Water-otter pup adopted from Isabela's estuary; can carry one spoken sentence inside a wave, undamaged, across any water. The party's unjammable courier — White Noise can flood every frequency, but not a wave's memory.
- **Stormcrown (雷, SR, L45)** — Legendary storm beast, the wordless thunder that *precedes* language — awe itself. It has refused every summoner in recorded history because summoning is command, and awe cannot be commanded. In Ch10 Rafa doesn't summon it; he introduces himself. It comes because it was asked, not ordered — Rule 1's deepest echo.

### SHADOW — the Risen 「影から上がる」
Hunters who leveled from nothing — the family whose grammar *is* the player's grammar (XP, ranks, gates). Throughline: what do you owe the version of you that started at level one?

- **Minjae (影, N, L10)** — Seoul rookie, weakest hunter of his cohort, guided by the *dokkaebi* — Korea's rowdy goblins who reward the humble and prank the proud. He keeps a notebook of every word he learns; it becomes, by the finale, one of the saga's relics. Sen's peer and pace-setter: his level is always roughly the player's.
- **Aditi (弓, R, L24)** — Indian ranger from the Chota Nagpur forests, her bow-line descending from Arjuna's Gandiva — the bow of the archer whose defining test (in the Mahābhārata) was seeing *only the eye of the target*: total attention, the archer's form of meaning it. She never misses what she can name; she refuses to loose at anything she can't.
- **Tomas (盾, SR, L41)** — Brazilian summoner whose shadow-knight is the standing memory of Zumbi of Palmares — the historical leader of the great quilombo of escaped slaves, killed 1695, whose name Brazil never let die (November 20 is his national day). Tomas's shield 盾 is literal remembered resistance; the Hush has tried for three centuries to erase Zumbi and failed, because too many people mean his name every year.
- **Ravindra (王, SSR, L50)** — The Shadow Monarch, Indian, throne-grounded in Ashoka — the emperor who, after the horror of Kalinga, renounced conquest and had his edicts *carved in stone across the subcontinent* in the people's own Prakrit: history's first mass act of a king speaking directly, permanently, to everyone. Ravindra's kingship 王 is exactly that: sovereignty as the duty to make words outlast the king. He rules the finale's ground — Bhimbetka is within his old edict-lands.

### CHIBI — the Smallfolk 「小さき衆」
Tiny mascots keeping tiny words — the diminutives, the snack-words, the nonsense syllables parents invent. Throughline: the Hush ignores them as beneath notice; this is its costliest error.

- **Mochi (豆, N, L1)** — Snack gremlin, keeper of the small comfort-words. First creature to greet Sen. Stows away through the entire saga; eats a Hush-quotation in Ch12 (nonsense words can't be quoted — there's nothing to mean) and is briefly, gloriously, the most dangerous being alive.
- **Flan (甘, N, L11)** — Dessert keeper, custodian of the world's sweetness-words in every tongue (*doce, tatlı, ngọt, mithā*). Runs the *Loanword*'s galley from Ch3; morale-officer whose puddings are, canonically, load-bearing.
- **Sir Pebble (勇, R, L25)** — Tiny cardboard knight, keeper of 勇 — courage, which he demonstrates is not the absence of fear but its correct pronunciation. Knighted (self-, with a biscuit) at the Ch6 bathhouse; insists on guarding doors far too large for him, and in Ch12 guards the one that matters.
- **Nibbles (竜, SR, L40)** — World's smallest dragon, last of a line that has shrunk generation by generation *as dragon-words faded* — his size is a live graph of the world's belief in dragons. Every reader who learns 竜, in-world, buys Nibbles a millimeter. He does not get big in the finale (that would be a lie); he gets *heard*, roaring the first syllable of Mu's new name because a dragon's roar carries where voices don't.

## THE ROUTE — ONE CONNECTED JOURNEY (v2, canonical; chapters.ts implements this)

The party's physical journey is a single loop: **out of India east along the sutra-strand, then the long sea road west to where letters began, then the monsoon run home to where art began.** Consecutive chapters connect on the ground (each shows the travel leg); globally distant keepers arrive TO the party by lattice-walking — the party itself never teleports. The engines: the comet timetable (stolen Ch5, surrendered Ch8), the hunt for the Mouth ("where marks began"), and the gathering of keepers.

1. **Ch1 — Varanasi.** Shiva's city; Devika's unbroken flame. The party forms. Hand-off: the static riding the sutra-strand points east.
2. **Ch2 — Tokyo** (walked east along the sutra-strand — the real historical road words took, India→China→Japan; Cangjie whispered en route). Noise, not silence, is the beachhead. Hand-off: the living sky-threads all run west; the sea-roads are dying.
3. **Ch3 — Seoul → the westward voyage** (ferry Tokyo→Busan; the *Loanword* departs Incheon; South China Sea, Malacca, into the Indian Ocean). The Censor's Lethe-offer; the first casualty. Hand-off: refit at the Kestrel House, which has moved ahead of the war.
4. **Ch4 — Suez & Cairo** (Red Sea, the Canal): Émile's safehouse-in-exile; kinship-word offensive; Tenzin arrives half-erased, Zola flies in with the survey, Sunmi delivers the frozen word; Emberkit takes the coal, Tenzin blesses the casks — fire-rite and water-rite board together. Hand-off: north up the Levant coast.
5. **Ch5 — Byblos** (coastal run): defense of the alphabet's root; Anya steals the ledger page ("the Archivist"; a comet timetable). Hand-off: the Archivist's trail and the party's exhaustion both point north to Istanbul.
6. **Ch6 — Istanbul** (Aegean → Bosphorus): the hamam respite; Echo's theft; Grandfather Ash remembers Halime. Hand-off: bells cry for help from the southern sky.
7. **Ch7 — Lalibela** (Suez again, Red Sea south, highland trek): Kunle lattice-walks in from Lagos; the doubt-siege of Amara's bells broken. Hand-off: the timetable and Adriatic word agree — the Censor moves on Skadar; his seat is drowned beneath Lisbon.
8. **Ch8 — Skadar → Lisbon** (the long western run: Adriatic relief, Gibraltar, the Tagus): Dragan's wall-for-name choice wakes Meridian; the drowned library falls to saudade and the moon-mirror; the Censor stops, and surrenders the timetable: the Long Return within the year; the Mouth opens where marks began. Hand-off: the road home is the battlefield — east.
9. **Ch9 — the monsoon Indian Ocean** (Suez a third time, homeward run): Dao reads the rain; Tomas and Biscuit arrive, and the blind-spot chart spells Bhimbetka; Echo caught and taught one meant word; Halime defects along Ash's unsevered strand and tells Mu's whole story. Hand-off: landfall, India.
10. **Ch10 — Mumbai & the Konkan coast**: White Noise's last offensive against the unwritten books (the oral Veda); Olamide brings Timbuktu's dispersal method — the party becomes a library; Haru's awakening; Rafa introduces himself to Rudra-thunder (Stormcrown). Hand-off: inland, 500 km to the painted rocks; the comet is visible.
11. **Ch11 — the high sky** (flown vertically from the Deccan up the last lit strand): The Pattern's structure-test; the comet's testimony; the empty grammatical slot. Hand-off: descent onto Bhimbetka as sixty threads knot.
12. **Ch12 — Bhimbetka**: the false pralaya's Mouth; the ring of sixty kanji; the naming of 間 *ma* — the rest-note of Shiva's drum restored. Epilogue at the campfire; one far strand hums for Saga Two.

Thematic braids across hand-offs: flame→coal (Ch1→4→12); noise→sea-silence (Ch2→3); water blessed in Ch4 quenches Ch5's quotation-fire; letters→tongues-in-steam (Ch5→6); bell-echo→wall-stones (Ch7→8); rain→storm (Ch9→10); comet-light→dawn-door (Ch11→12).

### CH.1 — 名前が消えた夜 *Namae ga Kieta Yoru* — "The Night the Names Went Out"
**Gate: L1–4.** Debuts: Devika, Little Yam, Mochi (L1); Bjorn, Tavita (L2); Renji, Amaya (L3); Idris, Pip (L4). Plus Sen, Tsuru.
**Setting:** Varanasi — the Manikarnika ghat and its unbroken cremation fire.
**Mythology:** Agni as carrier of meant speech; kotodama introduced as the system's ground truth.
**Beats:** Sen, an ordinary learner, wakes to find their street's name blank on every sign — and is the only one who notices. Tsuru arrives, folded and singed, with one word: *listen*. First Lattice-walk (Sen pays the toll: the memory of their childhood front door — the cost rule established on page one). Varanasi: Devika's flame is dimming not because the fire fails but because the *mourners are forgetting the names they came to speak*. The eight other keepers of the band arrive along converging strands — a deliberately global first party. The Hush's first on-page act: it takes an old mourner's word for "son." Sen, untrained, simply *learns the man's son's name from him and says it back* — and the strand relights. Devika names the war and the win-condition: 「名前のないものに、名前を。」
**Kanji spotlighted:** 言・炎・森・豆・壁・夢・斬・星・呪・心.
**Emotional turn:** Sen stops asking "why me" the moment relighting works — horror becomes vocation in a single panel.
**Key panels:** (1) Street sign with a licked-blank space where the name was, Sen's reflection in the metal — the only face looking. (2) Manikarnika at dusk: the eternal flame guttering *green-edged*, Devika kneeling with her palm inside it, unburned. (3) Nine strangers stepping out of nine threads of light onto the ghat steps, each thread a different color, the Ganges black behind them. (4) Sen speaking the son's name: the panel *re-inks* around the mourner — background detail returning line by line. (5) Devika writing 炎 in the air with an ember; the stroke order visible as afterglow.

**Setting:** Tokyo (Shibuya/Akihabara), with strand-scenes in Lima's fog and the Harz fog.
**Mythology:** Kotodama at home; Illapa's thunder-voice; the Brocken spectre as herald.
**Beats:** The party follows the Hush to the loudest city on earth and learns Rule 2's corollary from Diego: unmeant repetition is an open door — ads, feeds, slogans. White Noise debuts (voice only), hollowing a pop song until a crowd sings words none of them mean. Haru refuses to speak for two-thirds of the chapter; when a static-wave nearly takes Pip's "papa," Haru says his first full sentence of the saga and the street's screens all crack. Elias's shadow arrives at the meeting before he does (Brocken herald); Jun charts which sky-strands still stand; Rafa and his tonal-sense identify hollowed people by their missing animal-shadow.

**Emotional turn:** the party realizes the enemy's beachhead isn't silence — it's *noise*; the danger map inverts.
**Key panels:** (1) Shibuya crossing where every screen shows a mouth, none synced. (2) A crowd singing, speech bubbles all *empty outlines*. (3) Haru's single sentence rendered as one enormous hand-lettered bubble occupying a full page. (4) Elias's giant fog-shadow bowing before tiny Elias appears.

### CH.3 — 忘れられた海図 *Wasurerareta Kaizu* — "The Forgotten Charts"
**Gate: L10–14.** Debuts: Minjae (L10), Flan (L11), Isabela (L12), Cora (L13), Noor (L14).

Cora's woven charts + Noor's moon-road navigation get the *Loanword* through a stretch of ocean with no names left; Isabela sings a hollowed fishing village's memory back (Iara's song, run in reverse). Refused — but a crewman accepts, and they lose him mid-sentence, the saga's first casualty.

**Emotional turn:** the war has a body count, and its weapon is an *offer*.

(3) The crewman fading mid-laugh, his speech bubble finishing without him. (4) Noor walking a moonlight road across black water, Khonsu's crescent as the road's source.

### CH.4 — 家という言葉 *Ie to Iu Kotoba* — "The Word for Home"

**Setting:** Paris safehouse; Yamdrok Yumtso, Tibet; the Drakensberg escarpment.

**Beats:** The Hush opens its kinship-word offensive (Rule 1 workaround: isolate families, and the words starve). Émile's safehouse shelters refugees who've lost the word for "home" — Pip hears their meaning still intact underneath, proving *meaning survives its word* (the finale's keystone, planted here). Tenzin, nearly erased (Rule 3 down to one ferryman's memory of him), is found because Zola's aerial survey spots the *hole* in the Lattice shaped like a man. Sunmi freezes a dying grandmother's last word mid-air until her emigrant grandson can arrive to receive it. Emberkit takes the coal from Devika's flame: the first fire goes mobile.
**Kanji:** 父・霊・水・火・翼・氷.
**Emotional turn:** quietest chapter, biggest stakes — the party stops fighting *for the world* and starts fighting for *specific kitchens, specific names*.
**Key panels:** (1) Refugee family at Émile's table, empty bubble above them, while Pip's telepathy shows the same panel *in color* beneath. (2) Yamdrok from above: a turquoise lake with a man-shaped darkness on its shore. (3) Sunmi's frozen word hanging in lamplight like a paper crane of ice. (4) Emberkit carrying the coal in its mouth, tail shielding it from rain.

### CH.5 — 文字のふるさと *Moji no Furusato* — "Birthplace of Letters"

Ingrid splices the Byblos strand at the cost of feeling, for a day, every goodbye ever said along it. **Emotional turn:** the enemy has an org chart and a schedule; dread becomes strategy.

(3) Priya's blade mid-cut through a *named* curse, the name burning along the edge. (4) Anya on a night rooftop holding the ledger-page, her own crossed-out childhood entry visible.

### CH.7 — 嘘つきの真言 *Usotsuki no Shingon* — "The Con Man's True Word"
**Gate: L30–34.** Debuts: Baba Kunle (L30), Tidepup (L31), Amara (L34).
**Setting:** Lagos, then the rock churches of Lalibela, Ethiopia.
**Mythology:** Èṣù, orisha of crossroads and messages; àṣẹ; Lalibela and the Ge'ez chant tradition.
**Beats:** The intel points to Africa's oldest continuously used script. In Lagos, Idris confronts his uncle Kunle — the fraud who made him distrust words — and the chapter's theological grenade goes off: the Hush *cannot* feed on Kunle's cons, because kindly meant lies are still meant (Rule 1). Èṣù's lesson: the crossroads god deals in messages, not truths. At Lalibela, the Hush besieges Amara's bells with its cruelest weapon yet — not erasing the chants, but making the chanters *doubt they ever understood them*. Kunle, of all people, breaks the siege: a con man knows exactly how doubt is installed, so he uninstalls it. Amara rings the great bell; Tidepup carries her chant inside a wave down the Blue Nile's strand; three dark provinces relight.
**Kanji:** 師・波・鈴.
**Emotional turn:** the saga forgives its liar — and distinguishes, permanently, deception from unmeaning.
**Key panels:** (1) Lagos crossroads at noon, Èṣù's shadow pointing four ways at once. (2) Bet Giyorgis church seen from above, cross-shaped in living rock, Hush-fog pooling in the trench around it. (3) Kunle mid-patter, his speech bubble *warm-colored* among grey doubt. (4) The bell struck: sound rendered as concentric rings of Ge'ez and kanji interleaved.

### CH.8 — 沈んだ書庫 *Shizunda Shoko* — "The Drowned Library"

**Setting:** Skadar fortress, then beneath Lisbon — an archive drowned since the 1755 earthquake, now the Censor's seat.

**Beats:** War chapter. Kojo, reduced to his kra, carries the party's plan through dead strands when White Noise jams everything. He does not die; he *stops* — handing over the timetable: the Long Return arrives within the year, and "the Mouth opens where marks began." His last kept word, revealed: the name of the first library he failed to save.
**Kanji:** 巨・月・域・零・壁 (fallen)・忘 (foreshadow).
**Emotional turn:** the first lieutenant defeated by *being accurately remembered* — the finale's method, proven on a person.
**Key panels:** (1) Skadar's wall collapsing while the grandmother's name rises off the stones as light. (2) The drowned library: shelves under green water, every book pristine, fish swimming through unreadable titles. (3) Kojo as a single spark crossing a black, severed Lattice. (4) The Censor kneeling in Mateus's domain, surrounded by an absence shaped like everything he erased. (5) Meridian's stone eye, opening, seen only by the reader.

### CH.9 — 雨は空を読む *Ame wa Sora o Yomu* — "Rain Reads the Sky"

Echo, cornered at Teotihuacan trying to steal Nayeli's sky-word, is instead *caught* — and taught: Sen sits with him and teaches him one word the slow way, the first thing he has meant in years, and every stolen word in him hollows to ash except that one. He turns, bringing the Court's supply-map with him. The Archivist Hollow arrives not to fight but because Grandfather Ash's memory of her (Ch6) reached her along a strand she thought severed — Rule 3 as redemption mechanic. **Key panels:** (1) Naga fireballs rising off the Mekong at dusk, Dao reading them like lines of text. (2) Zola's chart with Biscuit's blind spots inked in — the gaps forming a word. (3) Sen and Echo cross-legged on the Pyramid of the Sun, one word passing between them like a coal. (4) Halime unhooding: an old librarian, not a monster, library-dust in her hollow like snow.

### CH.10 — 語られぬ書物のために *Katararenu Shomotsu no Tame ni* — "For the Unwritten Books"
**Gate: L44–45.** Debuts: Olamide (L44), Haru △ + Stormcrown (L45). White Noise falls (card unlocks L45 post-chapter).
**Setting:** Timbuktu and the Sahara's edge — the manuscript libraries and the griot lines of the Mande world.
**Mythology:** Sundiata's epic and the griots; the historical smuggling of Timbuktu's manuscripts to safety; awe (Stormcrown) as pre-language.
**Beats:** The Hush's last offensive before the comet: annihilate the *oral* — the epics, lineages, and songs that exist only in living memory, the Lattice's most vulnerable and most meant strands. Olamide holds Timbuktu as his ancestors held the manuscripts: by dispersal — every keeper memorizes a share (the party literally becomes a library). White Noise unleashes total static; Haru finally hears what he's feared his whole life, everyone speaking and no one meaning — and awakens by inversion: one complete, unfiltered, meant sentence, and the static shatters (覚). Rafa walks into the storm's eye and *introduces himself* to Stormcrown; thunder answers a request where it refused every command. White Noise, unhollowed at the end, remembers his name for one panel before dissolving — and it is written down, in Minjae's notebook, where the reader can find it.
**Kanji:** 覇・覚・雷.
**Emotional turn:** the quietest keeper and the loudest sky both choose meaning; the Court is broken.
**Key panels:** (1) Keepers seated in a circle in a sand-floored courtyard, each *becoming* a book — text flowing from manuscript pages up their arms. (2) Full-spread static: every panel border dissolving — except one small clear panel where Haru breathes. (3) Haru's sentence: a single page, one bubble, and the desert silent around it. (4) Rafa, tiny, hand raised to a storm the size of the sky: 「はじめまして。」 *Hajimemashite.* (5) A name written in a school notebook, rain-spotted.

### CH.11 — 千二百年目の帰還 *Sen-nihyaku-nen-me no Kikan* — "The Long Return"
**Gate: L46–47.** Debuts: The Long Return (L46), The Pattern (L47).
**Setting:** The high sky and the edge of it — Nayeli's serpent flying the last lit strand above the Pacific night.
**Mythology:** Quetzalcoatl as sky-road; the comet as witness; grammar older than words.
**Beats:** Bridge chapter, deliberately short and vertical. The vanguard rides to meet the comet whose light left before the forgetting — the only surviving *witness* to the first word's sky. En route, The Pattern manifests and tests the fellowship in the one language it speaks: structure. It rearranges the party's syntax — swaps their voices, their grammar, their pronouns — and watches whether meaning survives translation into pure form. Pip passes for everyone: meaning, heard directly, doesn't move when the words do (Ch4's keystone, weight-bearing at last). Satisfied, The Pattern grants the finale's instrument: an empty grammatical slot — a *place in language* where a new name can legally live. Tsuru, made of paper, makes the kami 紙/神 pun to a geometric god; The Pattern does not laugh, but adjusts one angle, which Tsuru insists counts. The comet's testimony arrives as light: the sky of 1,200 years ago, and under it, faint, the mouth-shape of the first word.
**Kanji:** 彗・神・紙.
**Emotional turn:** awe without terror — for one chapter, the war pauses and the universe is simply old and listening.
**Key panels:** (1) The feathered serpent climbing a single thread of light out of the cloud deck, cities glowing through cloud like embers. (2) The Pattern: the party's speech bubbles rearranged into a perfect lattice, their faces unchanged. (3) Comet-light falling on upturned faces — rendered as the *negative* of every dark panel so far. (4) A single empty bracket of light held in Sen's hands: a slot where a name will go.

### CH.12 — 名を呼べ *Na o Yobe* — "Say Its Name"
**Gate: L48–50.** Debuts: Sable, Seraphine (L48); Aurelio, Vesna (L49); Meridian, Bruna, Ravindra (L50). Mu (granted at completion).
**Setting:** The Bhimbetka rock shelters of Madhya Pradesh, India — real home of some of humanity's oldest rock art, inside Ashoka's old edict-lands — where the Mouth opens into the Unwritten Sea.
**Mythology:** Everything converges: Janus's threshold (Aurelio opens the Mouth at dawn); Ankou and Sable's court (even erasure is owed a hearing); Seraphine's lunar register (Mu's blank-but-entered line is the legal proof a name is *owed*); Vesna and Morana (the spring-rite relit on the shelter floor turns a lieutenant home); Ashoka's edicts (Ravindra holds sovereignty over the ground where words were first cut in stone); the returned sword (the Golden Turtle's summons: Bao's blade goes back mid-battle, because the war is *ending*, and he smiles).
**Beats:** All sixty keepers stand at Bhimbetka — the first time every strand of the cast knots. Aurelio opens the dawn-door; the vanguard descends into the Unwritten Sea, a whiteness where speech bubbles cannot form. Bruna carries the naming-brush (no flesh survives the cold of unmeaning; chosen steel does). Mu manifests at last as what it is: a person-shaped absence, quoting the party's own dead — its only voice. The battle is a siege of meaning per Rule 1: keepers hold a perimeter of *meant* words (each family voicing its kanji — the panel where all sixty glyphs burn in a ring is the saga's poster image) while Sen does the only thing Sen has ever done. Pip hears Mu's underneath: not hatred — the pain of an unmeant word, 1,200 years long. Rafa waves hello. Mochi eats a quotation. Nibbles roars the first syllable. And Sen — using the Pattern's slot, the comet's testimony, Seraphine's blank register line, and Sable's verdict that a hearing requires a name — *names it*: not a killing word but the word it lost, re-learned from the comet's light and re-meant by a living mind. The name is given on the last page in Japanese, romaji, and English, taught to the reader in the exact way the app teaches — the reader learns the saga's final vocabulary item *as the act that ends the war*. Mu, named, becomes finite: the keeper of 無 — the silence between words, the rest that gives speech its shape. The Hush ends not as an execution but as an adoption. Epilogue: relit world with honest scars (words erased before the naming stay lost unless relearned — the app's ongoing loop is the world's ongoing repair); Minjae's notebook enters Seraphine's register; Devika's flame burns ordinary orange; and one strand of the Lattice, far past the comet's orbit, hums with a signal in no known tongue. *Saga Two.*
**Kanji:** all sixty in the ring; then 無 — taught, finally, as a word like any other.
**Emotional turn:** triumph as *mercy performed with precision* — the shonen finale where the finishing move is comprehension.
**Key panels:** (1) Sixty keepers among Bhimbetka's painted rocks at first light, cave art and living cast in one continuous mural. (2) The ring of sixty burning kanji around a person-shaped whiteness. (3) Pip and Rafa before Mu — a child's wave answered, hesitantly, by an absence learning a gesture. (4) Sen writing the name with Bruna's brush: the stroke order shown across four micro-panels, the reader's eye forced to *learn it*. (5) Final page: Mu, small now, seated at the edge of the campfire circle — the silence between everyone's words, at last where it belongs.

## HOW PER-CARD MANGA PANELS DERIVE FROM CHAPTERS

Every card's page in the collection shows the **debut panel** from its band's chapter — the exact panel where that character first acts (not first appears in background). Rules for the art pipeline:

1. Debut panel = the character's *defining verb* (Devika: hand in the flame; Sunmi: freezing the word; Rafa: the wave). The key-panel lists above mark most of them; the remainder follow the same beat-verbs described in each cast entry.
2. The card's kanji must be visible *diegetically* in its debut panel (on a blade, bell, sail, screen, stone) — the card art teaches the glyph by exposure before the app ever glosses it.
3. SSR debut panels are full-page spreads from Ch11–12; SR are half-pages; R/N are standard panels — rarity maps to page real estate, which maps to narrative weight, so the gacha hierarchy and the story hierarchy are the same fact.
4. New-character cards (Echo, Halime, the Censor, White Noise, Mu) use their *turn* panel, not their menace panel — the collection is a record of who people became.
